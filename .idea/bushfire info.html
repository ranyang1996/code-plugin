Victoria - the most state affected by bushfires

Extreme fire weather has increased since the 1970s. Over the past 100 years, global surface air temperatures have risen by almost 1°C. Both the atmosphere and the oceans have warmed. Climate change is now making hot days hotter, and heatwaves longer and more frequent in Australia’s southeast. Severe long term rainfall deficits remain across much of western Victoria
and West and South Gippsland, which increase bushfire season risk. Very wet winter and spring conditions have led to high levels of grass growth and above-average fuel loads, particularly in the north of the state. Victoria has sustained
around half of the Australian economic losses from bushfires despite comprising only 3% of continent.

Influence on farming communities


Bushfires have the most serious effect in farming communities in many ways, from those whose homes are damaged or destroyed to those who lose property, stock, crops, water supplies, soils or pastures.

• Digital Agriculture Services, found that, 161717 ha of agricultural land in Victoria was located within fire boundaries.63% of the fire-attected area in VIC, NSW, SA, and TAS is experiencing a 1-in-20-year rainfall deficit

• Livestock, such as Cattle (beef and dairy), sheep, goats, horses, pigs, poultry, bee hives all suffer from bushfire. When bushfire happens, farms are difficult tomove
stock off an affected property.

• Fires do not only cause physical harm. Many people suffer from mental trauma from the experience of emergency
evacuation,farm damage, livestock or other sources of livelihoods.

• Black Saturday bushfires in Victoria claimed 173 lives, destroyed more than 2000 properties, burnt nearly 400,000 ha and affected 78 communities. The estimated cost of the Black Saturday bushfires to Victoria was approximately $4.4 billion.

The state is getting warmer and drier, we will experience harsher fire weather and longer fire seasons, rising sea level, more hot days in a year and more acidic oceans. Extreme temperatures are likely to increase at a similar rate to average temperature. If the current rate of global warming continues, Victorian towns could experience around double the number of very hot days each year by 2050.
